\begin{abstract}
This long-form critique synthesizes six focused reproduction studies of the Wide View Filter (WVF), Line Filter (LF), and later spline-angle / multi-scale follow-on papers. The mathematical core of the method survives scrutiny: the rotated Taylor least-squares formulation recovers known derivatives to machine precision, and the associated systems remain solvable even as conditioning worsens at larger support sizes. The empirical story is narrower. Exact 1001-threshold A100 benchmark runs show that support-matched classical baselines remain very strong: on BSDS500, Sobel-15x15 reaches ODS/OIS/AP 0.5491/0.5550/0.4682, ahead of DexiNed at 0.4950/0.5058/0.3990; on UDED, Sobel-9x9 reaches 0.9039/0.9151/0.9543, slightly above DexiNed at 0.9010/0.9102/0.9068. A direct 60-row angle sweep also fails to reproduce a blanket spline-angle advantage: moderate support can help, but small support often regresses and very large support is expensive. The completed 112-core WVF/LF scale sweeps sharpen that conclusion rather than rescuing it: on a five-image UDED subset, the best LF setting reaches ODS 0.2000 only on 64$\times$64 resized inputs and costs about 2972 s/img, while BSDS500 remains at ODS 0.0000 across all 40 completed WVF/LF configurations. The compute audit shows why: the present WVF/LF implementation is CPU-only NumPy/SciPy code with nested least-squares solves, and the new 112-core SLURM parallelization only improves sweep throughput, not kernel cost. The maritime motivation remains credible but bounded because the current real maritime image folder contains zero-byte placeholders and the four-image aquatic set from the source papers is not locally available. The resulting conclusion is conditional rather than dismissive: WVF/LF is mathematically legitimate and occasionally promising, but the strongest claims about benchmark superiority, spline-angle dominance, and practical deployment outrun the evidence currently reproduced in this repository.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The WVF/LF line of work proposes an orientation-aware alternative to conventional image derivatives for aquatic and low-visibility scenes. Across the thesis and the two OCEANS papers, the method evolves from a rotated two-dimensional Taylor expansion into a spline-based angle estimator and then into a multi-scale, multi-domain pipeline with Gaussian-mixture fusion~\cite{bagan2023thesis,bagan2024oceans,bagan2025oceans}. The motivation is not fabricated: maritime and underwater scenes are genuinely difficult because low illumination, water texture, scattering, and weak object boundaries make edge localization unstable~\cite{marques2019,ji2023}.

The core problem with the published story is not that it is obviously false. It is that several strong claims were stated more broadly than the current evidence can justify. A method can be mathematically coherent while still being benchmarked unfairly. A visual result can be interesting while still depending on missing data. An angle estimator can be better in some parameter regimes and worse in others. The right critique therefore has to be scope-aware rather than rhetorical.

This revision is organized around that scope question. It synthesizes six focused substudies written from the latest local evidence base: source-paper boundary analysis, exact BSDS500 benchmarking, exact UDED benchmarking, spline-angle reassessment, compute/practicality audit, and maritime claims analysis. All analyses and paper builds were submitted through SLURM on the Theia cluster. The result is narrower and stronger than the earlier draft. Instead of asking whether WVF/LF is ``good'' in the abstract, this paper asks which claims are directly reproduced, which are only partially supported, and which still depend on data or code that are absent from the repository.

\section{Source-Paper Scope and Reproduction Boundary}
The first correction is to state precisely what the repository does and does not reproduce. The thesis, the 2024 OCEANS paper, and the later multi-scale OCEANS paper mix several kinds of claims: mathematical claims about the WVF solve, empirical claims about BSDS500/UDED/BIPED benchmarks, visual claims about aquatic imagery, and systems claims about spline angles and multi-scale fusion. Those claims do not all stand on the same footing locally.

Table~\ref{tab:scope} makes the core mismatch visible. The repository now reproduces several important ingredients that were previously missing, especially exact-threshold BSDS500 and UDED runs on A100 hardware and a direct angle sweep. But it still does not contain the BIPED dataset, the four-image aquatic set discussed in the source papers, or the later multi-scale Gaussian-mixture fusion implementation. Those are not peripheral omissions. They are exactly the pieces needed to support the strongest end-to-end claims.

The same point applies to parameter scale. The source papers emphasize larger support sizes such as $N_p=150$ and $N_p=250$, and LF line widths corresponding to $m=14$. Earlier repository artifacts were limited to much smaller settings, especially $N_p=15$ and $m=3$. The finished 112-core defq sweeps now close that ``still running'' loophole on resized five-image subsets: they cover $N_p \in \{15,50,100,150,250\}$, LF half-widths $m \in \{3,7,14\}$, and two resize regimes ($64\times64$ and $96\times96$). The final outcome does not rescue the strongest source-paper claims. It instead sharpens the reproduction boundary: some LF settings improve tiny UDED subsets modestly, but BSDS500 remains unrecovered and runtime remains extreme.

\section{Verified Mathematical Core}
\subsection{Taylor-Expansion Formulation}
The mathematically strongest part of the source papers is the WVF local model. The method fits a rotated two-dimensional Taylor expansion to a neighborhood of $N_p$ samples:
\begin{equation}
f(x_i, y_i) \approx f^0 + f_x^0 x_i + f_y^0 y_i + \frac{f_{xx}^0 x_i^2}{2} + \frac{f_{yy}^0 y_i^2}{2} + f_{xy}^0 x_i y_i + \cdots
\label{eq:taylor}
\end{equation}
which yields an overdetermined linear system solved in least-squares form. That formulation is reproducible and coherent. On a known polynomial signal, the repository recovers all tested derivatives to machine precision.

This is the part of the WVF/LF story that should be credited without hedging. The local solver is not numerically vacuous. It is a real method with a defensible mathematical basis. The rest of the critique concerns what happens when that basis is turned into empirical performance claims.

\subsection{Numerical Stability}
The source papers rely on repeated local solves but do not discuss conditioning in much depth. The repository now does. Figure~\ref{fig:cond} and Table~\ref{tab:cond} show that the normal equations remain solvable across tested orientations, but the large-support regime is not numerically trivial. By $N_p=250$, the maximum condition number in the current study reaches $1.47 \times 10^5$.

They also record hardware provenance explicitly: the learned baselines run through PyTorch on CUDA when available, while classical filters remain CPU NumPy/SciPy code. That disclosure matters because it prevents the earlier mistake of talking about ``A100 benchmarking'' as if every method used the same execution path.

\subsection{BSDS500}
The BSDS500 exact run uses 50 images out of the 200-image test artifact available locally. Under that exact protocol, the strongest reproduced result is not a learned model and not a WVF/LF variant. It is Sobel-15x15, which reaches ODS/OIS/AP 0.5491/0.5550/0.4682 and remains faster than the learned baselines despite staying on CPU. DexiNed and TEED both trail it in quality and take 37.0 and 45.0 ms per image respectively on the A100-backed PyTorch path.

The methodological lesson is the important one. Support size changes the interpretation of the benchmark. If a comparison set contains only $3\times3$ Sobel or Prewitt kernels, then larger-neighborhood methods can appear exceptional for reasons that have as much to do with support as with orientation modeling. Once broader classical baselines are admitted, the story becomes much less flattering to broad superiority language.

\subsection{UDED}
The UDED exact run uses the full 30-image local artifact and is even more relevant to the source-paper framing because UDED is one of the central datasets invoked in the later WVF/LF papers. The top of the table is not a decisive learned-model win. Sobel-9x9 reaches ODS/OIS/AP 0.9039/0.9151/0.9543, while DexiNed reaches 0.9010/0.9102/0.9068. That is a near-tie in ODS and OIS, but not in AP, where the classical baseline holds a much larger lead. TEED trails both.

The correct reading is narrow but important: UDED does not support a simple ``modern CNNs dominate classical filters'' narrative under the exact local protocol. It also does not by itself prove WVF/LF superiority, because the exact UDED run does not include a full-resolution, paper-like WVF/LF evaluation. What it does is remove a convenient rhetorical shortcut. If the authors want to argue that orientation-aware Taylor fitting is decisively better, the control baselines need to survive a comparison against genuinely strong classical operators, not only minimal-support ones.

\section{Direct Angle Reassessment}
The later paper's strongest claim is not actually about ODS or OIS; it is about gradient orientation. The repository now has a direct synthetic angle sweep over five support sizes, three orientation discretizations, and four signal-to-noise levels, for 60 total rows. That sweep does not support a blanket claim of uniform spline superiority.

The best overall row in the current sweep is $N_p=50$, 18 orientations, SNR 0.75, where the spline estimate reduces mean angular error by 21.99 degrees at 1.70 seconds. \section{Compute Practicality and Scale Sensitivity}
The compute story is as important as the quality story. The current WVF/LF implementation in the repository is plain NumPy/SciPy code in \texttt{src/wvf\_lf.py}. It loops over pixels, orientations, and line positions, rebuilding many small least-squares systems. There is no CUDA kernel, no CuPy backend, and no low-level vectorized implementation for the inner solver. The benchmark harness is therefore heterogeneous by design: classical filters and WVF/LF run on CPU, while the learned baselines can use PyTorch on GPU when CUDA is available.

The new full-node SLURM work is still valuable. The sweep scripts now request exclusive 112-core defq nodes and distribute independent WVF/LF tasks across worker processes while pinning BLAS-related threading to one thread per worker. That is the correct way to use a large CPU node for an embarrassingly parallel sweep. It makes the cluster useful. It does \emph{not} make WVF/LF operationally light.

The final p112 sweeps sharpen the critique in a way the earlier serial evidence could only hint at. On UDED, the best LF row is not a paper-like triumph; it is a tiny, resized, five-image subset result at ODS 0.2000 and about 2971.59 seconds per image. The best WVF row on UDED is weaker still at ODS 0.1208 and 1610.97 seconds per image. Averaged over the whole 40-row UDED sweep, WVF reaches mean ODS 0.0836 and LF reaches mean ODS 0.0971. Those numbers are not competitive with the exact-threshold UDED Sobel and DexiNed baselines, which sit near 0.90 ODS on a full 30-image artifact.

BSDS500 is even more damaging to the strong-claim version of the WVF/LF story. The finished BSDS500 p112 sweep contains 40 rows spanning both resize regimes, all five support sizes, and all LF half-widths, and every single one of those rows finishes at ODS 0.0000 with OIS 0.0000 and AP 0.0000. This is not a matter of a few weak settings. It is a whole completed grid with no recovered BSDS500 signal at all.

That final result removes the last meaningful provisional caveat from the compute section. The source papers' larger-support settings were worth testing because one could reasonably imagine a regime in which broader support rescued quality enough to justify the extra cost. The completed sweeps show that this does not happen cleanly in the current reproduction. UDED produces a few modest LF gains on tiny resized subsets, but at enormous cost. BSDS500 does not recover at all. This is why the deployment and superiority claims have to be rewritten as conditional at best.

\section{Maritime and Aquatic Claims Boundary}
The maritime motivation in the source papers is credible, but it is bounded. The synthetic maritime scenes in the repository are useful stress tests: horizons, wave fields, cables, and underexposed marine scenes are genuinely awkward for edge detection. The repeated-trial synthetic benchmark therefore supports the problem framing. What it does not support is a broad claim of superiority on real maritime imagery, because the local real-image directory contains zero-byte placeholders and the four-image aquatic set from the source papers is not present.

The correct paper-level claim is therefore bounded: maritime and low-SNR scenes are a meaningful adversarial setting, but the current repository only supports a synthetic stress-test argument, not a broad real-image victory claim.

This table is the most important outcome of the long-form synthesis. The fair critique is not that the method is fraudulent or trivial. It is that the source-paper narrative compresses very different evidence categories into one story: verified mathematics, promising but parameter-sensitive empirical behavior, and still-unreproduced systems claims.

The next steps now follow from completed rather than pending evidence. First, the finished p112 sweeps should be treated as the current baseline sensitivity study, not as a placeholder. They already show that BSDS500 is unrecovered and that UDED gains, where present, are small and expensive. Second, any future comparison should keep support size matched whenever WVF/LF is compared to ``traditional'' operators. Third, the missing real-image evaluation path still needs to be recovered: BIPED, the four-image aquatic set, and the later multi-scale GMM implementation remain the largest unresolved holes in the reproduction boundary. Finally, if the authors want to make a practical-deployment argument, the method needs a faster inner-loop implementation, not just more SLURM workers or more patient batch queues.

\section{Conclusion}
The fairest conclusion is conditional rather than dismissive. WVF/LF is mathematically legitimate. The rotated Taylor model works. Moderate-support spline-angle estimation can help in some regimes. The maritime and low-SNR motivation is real. Those are genuine strengths, and the method deserves credit for them.

What does not currently survive reproduction is the stronger package of claims around broad benchmark superiority, uniformly better angle estimation, and operational practicality. Exact 1001-threshold A100 runs show that large-support Sobel baselines remain very strong on BSDS500 and UDED. The angle sweep is mixed rather than monotone. The completed 112-core scale sweeps now add a harder empirical limit: BSDS500 remains at ODS 0.0000 across the full finished WVF/LF paper-like grid, while the best UDED LF row only reaches ODS 0.2000 on a tiny resized subset at roughly 2972 seconds per image. The current implementation is CPU-only and expensive. The most distinctive empirical pieces of the published story - BIPED, the four-image aquatic set, and the later multi-scale GMM fusion system - remain outside the local reproduction boundary.

That is the right long-form reading of the current repository. The method is not empty hype, but neither is it fully validated by the evidence that is actually reproduced here. Until the missing data, missing pipeline components, and missing performance engineering are closed, the strongest claims should be rewritten as narrower, conditional statements.
